### [LP-NavOA: Humanoid Navigation Under Limited Perception](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.23249v1) ⭐️ 8.0/10

LP-NavOA proposes a limited-perception navigation and obstacle avoidance framework for humanoid robots that integrates a learned locomotion backbone with a distilled recurrent local planner to handle sparse goals and occlusions. This framework significantly improves on-time arrival rates from 38-40% to 85-97% in cluttered environments, addressing key challenges for real-world humanoid deployment such as obstacle avoidance and goal recovery under limited perception. The system uses a raycast-conditioned PPO locomotion backbone trained with a circular heading-speed command, and a distilled recurrent local planner that overwrites only the heading command at runtime, requiring no global map or external planner.

rss · arXiv Humanoid Robotics · Jun 22, 12:31

**Background**: Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) is a reinforcement learning algorithm that stabilizes policy updates via a clipping mechanism. Knowledge distillation transfers knowledge from a teacher model (e.g., A* planner) to a smaller student model (e.g., recurrent local planner) for efficient deployment.

### [TEXEDO: Test-Time Scaling for Humanoid Motion Generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.22998v2) ⭐️ 8.0/10

TEXEDO introduces a test-time scaling framework that selects physically executable motions from a pretrained text-conditioned generator by using a reward model with a dynamic feasibility verifier distilled from whole-body tracking rollouts. This bridges the gap between semantically plausible motions and physically executable ones for humanoid robots, enabling more reliable deployment of language-guided motion generation in real-world scenarios. The framework treats dynamic feasibility as a hard constraint and semantic alignment as the selection objective within the feasible set, and has been validated through large-scale simulations and real-world deployment on a Unitree G1 humanoid robot.

rss · arXiv Humanoid Robotics · Jun 22, 08:14

**Background**: Text-conditioned motion generation for humanoid robots typically uses human motion datasets retargeted to robot morphologies, which provide rich semantic priors but fail to capture controller-specific dynamics like balance and contact forces. Test-time scaling is a technique that improves model performance by allocating additional computation at inference time, often through sampling multiple outputs and selecting the best one. TEXEDO applies this idea to humanoid motion generation by using a reward model that verifies both physical feasibility and semantic alignment.

### [FT-WBC: Fault-Tolerant Whole-Body Control for Legged Manipulators](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.24466v1) ⭐️ 7.0/10

FT-WBC introduces a fault-tolerant whole-body control framework for legged manipulators that handles actuator failures by decoupling upper- and lower-body policies and using a Fault Estimator and Posture Adaptation Module. This work addresses a critical gap in robust loco-manipulation, as actuator failures can cause instability and task failure in legged manipulators. The framework improves survival rate and workspace under failures, enhancing safety and reliability for real-world deployment. The Fault Estimator predicts faulty joints from lower-body proprioceptive histories, while the Posture Adaptation Module adapts the base posture plan to convert unstable requests into safe commands. Experiments show significant improvements in survival rate and workspace under weakening or locked failures, with zero-shot transfer to a real robot.

rss · arXiv Humanoid Robotics · Jun 23, 11:58

**Background**: Legged manipulators combine legged mobility with robotic arm manipulation, but arm-induced center-of-mass shifts and disturbances make them prone to instability under actuator failures. Existing fault-tolerant control methods focus on locomotion alone, leaving the coupled whole-body stability and arm reachability problem unaddressed. Whole-body control (WBC) coordinates all joints for locomotion and manipulation, but typically assumes healthy actuators.
